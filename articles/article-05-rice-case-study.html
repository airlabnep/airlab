Case Study: Rice Management at NARC Research Station

By Field Operations TeamApril 15, 20246 min read

Background

Rice is one of Nepal's most important staple crops, providing food security and livelihoods for millions. The Nepal Agricultural Research Council (NARC) Research Station in Khumaltar has been conducting research on rice production for decades. This case study describes a precision agriculture project implementing geospatial technologies and data-driven decision making on NARC's experimental rice fields.

Objectives

  • Assess spatial variability in soil properties and rice growth across research fields
  • Develop site-specific management recommendations
  • Compare precision management with conventional uniform management
  • Validate remote sensing indices for rice monitoring in Nepal conditions

Methodology

Three rice fields at NARC Khumaltar (total 2.5 hectares) were included in the study. Soil sampling was conducted on a 20m grid, measuring pH, organic matter, available nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium. Baseline multispectral drone imagery was collected at planting, and follow-up surveys at 15-day intervals through the growing season. NDVI and NDRE indices were calculated for each survey.

One field was managed conventionally (uniform water management and fertilizer application). Two fields received precision management: one based on soil test results only, another based on both soil tests and mid-season drone monitoring. All fields received the same rice variety and received similar management otherwise.

Results

Soil Variability

Soil test results revealed significant spatial variation: available nitrogen ranged from 180 to 320 kg/hectare, phosphorus from 15 to 42 ppm, and potassium from 140 to 280 ppm. Management zones were delineated based on soil test results, with high-nutrient zones receiving lower inputs and low-nutrient zones receiving higher inputs.

Crop Performance

The conventionally managed field showed NDVI values of 0.60-0.65 at maximum canopy development. Precision management fields showed more variation: high-fertility zones reached NDVI of 0.68-0.72, while lower fertility zones in the same field showed NDVI of 0.58-0.63. Mid-season drone monitoring allowed identification of underperforming areas, with topdressing nitrogen applied to areas with NDVI below 0.60, resulting in improved development.

Yield Results

  • Conventional field: 4.8 tons/hectare (baseline productivity)
  • Soil-test-based precision: 5.4 tons/hectare (+12.5%)
  • Soil + drone-monitoring precision: 5.8 tons/hectare (+20.8%)

Economic Analysis

Input costs were reduced by optimizing fertilizer application: soil-based management reduced nitrogen and phosphorus inputs by 15%, while drone-based management was similarly efficient due to elimination of excessive fertilizer in already-adequate zones offsetting additional topdressing in deficient zones. Net cost reduction was approximately NPR 3,000-4,000 per hectare while yields increased significantly.

Key Learnings

1. Spatial variability is significant: Even within research fields perceived as homogeneous, substantial variation in soil properties and crop performance exists, justifying management zone approaches.

2. Remote sensing is powerful for mid-season correction: Identifying underperforming areas at boot stage allowed targeted interventions that significantly improved outcomes compared to predetermined management.

3. Integration improves results: Combining soil tests with in-season monitoring outperformed either approach alone, suggesting complementary value.

4. Scalability considerations: While beneficial on research station scale, the practical implementation requires farmer capacity building and affordable service options for small-hold farmers.

Recommendations for Extension

Based on these results, NARC has begun developing extension recommendations and training programs. Demonstration fields are being established in farmers' fields across Nepal's rice-growing regions to validate findings under diverse conditions. Training focuses on soil testing interpretation, basic drone operation and data collection, and remote sensing image analysis.

Conclusion

This case study demonstrates that precision agriculture approaches can significantly improve rice productivity and profitability while reducing input costs in the Nepal context. Combining soil tests with in-season remote sensing monitoring provides the most comprehensive approach. Scalable service models and farmer training are critical next steps to translate research findings into practical adoption by Nepal's farming community.
