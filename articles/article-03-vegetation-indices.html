Understanding Vegetation Indices: NDVI, NDRE, and Beyond

By Data Science Team April 28, 2024 7 min read

Introduction

Vegetation indices are among the most powerful tools in remote sensing for agricultural monitoring. These mathematical combinations of reflectance values from different wavelengths of electromagnetic radiation provide quantitative measures of vegetation health, biomass, and productivity. Understanding how to calculate, interpret, and apply various vegetation indices is fundamental to precision agriculture and environmental monitoring.

This comprehensive guide explains the theory behind the most widely used vegetation indices, their advantages and limitations, and practical applications in crop monitoring and agricultural decision-making.

Why Vegetation Reflects Light Differently

To understand vegetation indices, we first need to understand how healthy plants interact with light. The spectral reflectance of vegetation results from the biochemical and structural properties of leaves:

Chlorophyll Absorption: Chlorophyll in healthy leaves strongly absorbs visible light (400-700 nm), particularly in the blue (450 nm) and red (650 nm) regions of the spectrum. This absorbed light powers photosynthesis. As chlorophyll content decreases (due to nitrogen deficiency, stress, or senescence), less visible light is absorbed, and more is reflected.

Near-Infrared Reflectance: Healthy green leaves reflect a high proportion of near-infrared light (700-1300 nm) due to the cellular structure of the mesophyll. This reflection serves no photosynthetic function but is diagnostic of healthy leaf tissue. The sharp transition between red absorption and near-infrared reflection (the "red edge") is a hallmark of healthy vegetation.

Water and Dry Matter: The position of the red-edge and reflectance in the shortwave infrared (1400-2400 nm) provide information about leaf water content and dry matter accumulation.

The Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI)

Formula and Calculation

The most widely used vegetation index is NDVI, calculated as:

NDVI = (NIR - Red) / (NIR + Red)

Where NIR is reflectance in the near-infrared band (typically 800-900 nm) and Red is reflectance in the red band (typically 650 nm). The formula normalizes the difference by the sum, producing a dimensionless index ranging from -1 to +1.

Interpretation

NDVI values indicate vegetation health:

  • 0.7 to 1.0: Very healthy, dense vegetation (forests, well-managed crops)
  • 0.5 to 0.7: Healthy vegetation with moderate vigor (actively growing crops)
  • 0.3 to 0.5: Sparse vegetation or stressed plants (early growth, stressed crops)
  • 0.1 to 0.3: Very sparse or heavily stressed vegetation
  • Below 0.1: Non-vegetated areas (water, soil, buildings)

Advantages of NDVI

  • Simple to calculate and widely available
  • Works well for biomass estimation and yield prediction
  • Normalizes illumination differences and atmospheric effects reasonably well
  • Can be calculated from 2-band sensors (red and NIR only)

Limitations of NDVI

  • Saturates in dense vegetation, making it less sensitive to changes in very lush crops
  • Less sensitive to nitrogen status than some alternatives
  • Can be affected by soil background in sparse canopies
  • Not ideal for detecting early-season stress

NDRE and Red-Edge Vegetation Indices

The red-edge region (680-750 nm) shows a steep change in reflectance due to the chlorophyll absorption edge. Advanced multispectral sensors including a red-edge band enable calculation of indices more sensitive to chlorophyll content and nitrogen status than NDVI.

Normalized Difference Red-Edge Index (NDRE)

NDRE = (NIR - Red-Edge) / (NIR + Red-Edge)

NDRE is calculated similarly to NDVI but uses the red-edge band (740 nm) instead of the red band. This index is more sensitive to chlorophyll concentration and nitrogen content, particularly in mid-to-late growing season when NDVI may saturate. NDRE typically ranges from 0.2 to 0.8 for vegetation.

Other Red-Edge Indices

Chlorophyll Red-Edge Index (CIred-edge): Calculated as (NIR/Red-Edge) - 1, this index directly reflects red-edge reflectance and correlates highly with chlorophyll content.

Red-Edge Position (REP): The wavelength at which reflectance reaches 50% between the red minimum and NIR maximum. Plants with higher chlorophyll content show a shift in REP toward longer wavelengths. This requires spectral resolution to calculate but provides excellent sensitivity to nitrogen status.

Other Important Vegetation Indices

Green Chlorophyll Index (GCI)

GCI = (NIR/Green) - 1

Using the green band to normalize NIR, this index is highly correlated with leaf chlorophyll content. It's particularly useful for detecting nitrogen deficiency and is less affected by atmospheric conditions than indices using the red band. Values typically range from 0.5 to 2.0 for vegetated areas.

Enhanced Vegetation Index (EVI)

EVI = 2.5 × (NIR - Red) / (NIR + 6 × Red - 7.5 × Blue + 1)

EVI includes the blue band to correct for atmospheric aerosol effects and includes a soil adjustment factor. It avoids saturation better than NDVI in dense vegetation but requires three bands and is more complex to calculate. Commonly used for regional-scale vegetation monitoring.

Soil Adjusted Vegetation Index (SAVI)

SAVI = ((NIR - Red) / (NIR + Red + L)) × (1 + L)

SAVI includes a soil brightness correction factor (L, typically 0.5) to reduce the influence of exposed soil in sparse canopies. This makes it superior to NDVI in early growing season when soil is visible between plants.

Practical Application in Crop Monitoring

Early Season Monitoring

In early growth stages when canopy is sparse and soil visible, SAVI or simple green index may perform better than NDVI. Tracking indices through the season allows detection of areas developing slower than expected, enabling timely interventions.

Nitrogen Status Assessment

Red-edge based indices (NDRE, CIred-edge) are superior to NDVI for estimating plant nitrogen status. In mid-season, these indices can be related to tissue nitrogen concentration through regression models, enabling quantitative assessment of nitrogen adequacy.

Stress Detection

Plants under water stress initially show declining NDVI before visible symptoms appear, enabling early detection. Combining multiple indices improves accuracy - water stress affects blue and green wavelengths differently than nutrient stress.

Yield Prediction

Vegetation indices measured at anthesis correlate with grain yields. Studies show NDVI at flowering can predict maize yields with R² values exceeding 0.85. Time-integrated NDVI (area under the curve of NDVI over season) provides even stronger yield correlation.

Case Study: Field Comparison

A field in Chitwan district was monitored throughout the maize season. Drone surveys at V8, V12, V16, R1, and R3 growth stages calculated NDVI and NDRE.

Analysis revealed an area in the south field where NDVI remained below 0.6 throughout the season while NDRE showed values of only 0.4 at mid-season (vs 0.6-0.7 in healthy areas). This indicated chlorophyll deficiency despite moderate overall vigor. Ground investigation confirmed nitrogen deficiency in that zone. Variable rate nitrogen application in the following season resulted in improved performance in that area.

Limitations and Challenges in Index Use

While vegetation indices are powerful, several factors limit their use:

Atmospheric Effects: Cloud cover, aerosol loading, and water vapor affect reflectance and require atmospheric correction for accurate analysis. High-altitude multispectral satellites require more correction than low-altitude drones.

Sensor Differences: Different camera sensors have different spectral response curves. An NDVI value from one sensor may not be directly comparable to another sensor. Indices should only be compared if calculated from identically processed data.

Phenological Effects: The meaning of a given NDVI value changes throughout the season. Early-season NDVI of 0.5 is normal, while the same value at anthesis indicates stress. Stage-specific thresholds and baseline values are necessary.

Non-Vegetation Factors: Indices respond to vegetation but are also influenced by soil properties, residue, and other land cover. Pure vegetation indices may not perfectly isolate crop signal.

Conclusion

Vegetation indices are fundamental tools for translating spectral reflectance into agricultural meaning. NDVI remains widely used for its simplicity and reasonable performance, but red-edge indices like NDRE provide superior sensitivity to chlorophyll and nitrogen status. Understanding the strengths and limitations of different indices is essential for selecting the right tool for specific monitoring objectives. Modern multispectral sensors enable calculation of multiple complementary indices, with integrated analysis providing more robust assessment than any single index alone.
